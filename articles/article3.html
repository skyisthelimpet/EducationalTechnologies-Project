The Role of Video Games in the Classroom

When I was a teacher, I was always on the lookout for materials that my students would find interesting, relevant, and engaging. I was also a fan of activities that wouldn’t bring me untold misery or make me regret my life choices! The idea of using video games in the classroom used to simultaneously fill me with enthusiasm and dread.

The motivational aspect is obvious. And, as a lifelong gamer myself, I’m aware that video games can develop beneficial learning skills such as perseverance, curiosity, and problem-solving. But how do I integrate them into my classroom in practice? By using video games with my students, will I just be making my life more difficult for no good reason? The good news is using video games in class doesn’t have to be a headache because there’s so much flexibility when it comes to the approaches you can take.

I work as part of the team at Cambridge University Press & Assessment who create the Adventures in English with Cambridge video game series, designed to support students at beginner, elementary, and lower intermediate levels (A1, A2, and B1 on the CEFR). Here is an infographic to show some of the options available to you in using video games with your class:

Game Example 1: Crossy Road

Approach: One device per class

PEGI rating: 3+
Time: 45 mins approx.

You will need:

  • A touchscreen that can be screencast onto a projector, or simply a single touchscreen device.
  • A copy of the game. It’s available on the Google Play and App Store.

About the game: In Crossy Road, you are a chicken! Your job is to cross as many roads as you can without getting squashed by the traffic. Don’t worry – the squashing animations are very cute!

Lesson Plan

Start with a lead-in activity about jokes. Do the students know any jokes in English? You can introduce classics like "What kind of key do you need to open a banana?" (Answer: A monkey!) or "Why did the chicken cross the road?" to set the context.

Introduce the game, teaching phrases like "look both ways before crossing the road," "stop when the light is red," and "go when the light is green." Students can practice miming these actions before playing.

Demonstrate the game on a screencast-enabled touchscreen or use a rolling pair system where students take turns. The class can cheer or comment with phrases like "Be careful!" or "Cars are very fast!" Record each student’s score and find the winner.

Extension Ideas

Add more activities that build on road safety language, animal vocabulary, or joke creation. For example, ask students, "Why did the duck/giraffe cross the road?" and let them create their own punchlines.

Game Example 2: Gormi’s Winter Wonderland

Approach: In small groups or pairs

PEGI rating: 7+
Time: 45 mins approx.

You will need:

  • Devices for small groups/pairs that support Minecraft Bedrock edition.
  • Copies of the game. Find more information about it online.

About the game: Gormi’s Winter Wonderland focuses on cold weather and winter activities. Students help Gormi host a party by solving puzzles, exploring different rooms, and escaping a massive igloo.

Lesson Plan

Students work in pairs or small groups, taking turns playing the game and reading dialogue. Pre-teach key phrases like "What’s over there?" or "We have to go this way." Students can report back to the class about what they discovered in the game.

Extension Ideas

Break the game into segments for multiple lessons. Encourage students to collaborate on solving puzzles and using in-game vocabulary.

Final Thoughts

Incorporating video games into the classroom can provide engaging, memorable lessons that foster language skills like speaking, listening, reading, and writing. By carefully selecting games and using targeted strategies, educators can make learning enjoyable and effective.

If you’d like to know more, check out the 15-minute talk given at IATEFL 2019, which debunks myths about video games and highlights their benefits for language learning.
